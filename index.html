Anjelina Villalobos

Working at the volatile intersection of chaos and control, Anjelina Villalobos constructs visceral narratives through layered abstraction‚Äîwhere digital precision collides with gestural abandon.

Born in Los Angeles in 1989, Villalobos emerged from the early 2010s street art scene before pivoting to studio practice. Her work interrogates the tension between analog mark-making and computational systems, often building paintings through dozens of translucent layers that suggest depth, memory, and digital glitch artifacts.

Rather than depicting technology, she performs it‚Äîusing painting as a slow, deliberate response to the speed and compression of digital life. Each canvas becomes an archaeological site: traces of previous iterations bleed through, creating what she calls "temporal collisions."

"I'm not interested in making paintings about screens. I'm interested in making paintings that feel like what it's like to live with screens‚Äîfragmented, layered, haunted by what came before."

Her process is ritualistic. She begins each work digitally, generating algorithmic color palettes and compositional scaffolds, then abandons the screen entirely. The paintings emerge through weeks of additive and subtractive mark-making: pouring, scraping, sanding back to earlier layers, building up again. What remains is neither purely digital nor analog, but exists in productive tension between both.

Villalobos describes her practice as "painting after the internet"‚Äîa term she uses not chronologically, but conceptually. Her work acknowledges that painting can no longer pretend the digital realm doesn't exist, yet refuses to be subsumed by it.

"Every painting is a small rebellion against immediacy," she explains. "I'm drawn to processes that can't be undone with Command-Z. There's a permanence to analog error that I find generative. The digital allows infinite revisions; painting forces commitment."

Her studio practice incorporates meditation, long periods of observation, and intentional technological disconnection. She views each painting as a "slow artifact" in opposition to the velocity of contemporary image culture.
